ACCESS

Out of Kenya's population of roughly 56 million people, approximately 19 million do not have reliable access to clean, fresh water. Many of those affected live in dry rural regions, where the nearest water source can be several kilometers away. Women and children often bear the burden of daily water collection, walking long distances and sacrificing time that could otherwise be spent on education or economic activity.

SANITATION

Less than a third of Kenya's population has access to basic sanitation facilities such as toilets and handwashing stations. Open defecation remains common in rural and informal urban settlements, creating conditions that accelerate the spread of preventable diseases. The lack of proper sanitation also disproportionately affects girls and women, who face serious safety risks when forced to relieve themselves in the open, and who often drop out of school due to inadequate facilities.

CONTAMINATION

Waterborne diseases like cholera are among the leading causes of death for children under the age of five in Kenya. Contaminated water sources spread pathogens rapidly, especially in communities without access to treatment or filtration. Industrial runoff, agricultural chemicals, and improper waste disposal further degrade water quality, turning rivers and wells into health hazards rather than lifelines for the communities that depend on them.

ENVIRONMENT

Because of Kenya's limited optimal land for agriculture and dry climate, water scarcity is an issue. More than 80% of Kenya's land area is classified as arid or semi-arid, making it inherently difficult to sustain reliable water supplies for both people and agriculture. The country is increasingly vulnerable to prolonged droughts driven by climate change, which dry up rivers, deplete aquifers, and destroy crops.

URBANIZATION

Kenya is urbanizing at one of the fastest rates in sub-Saharan Africa, with cities like Nairobi growing well beyond the capacity of existing water and sewage infrastructure. Informal settlements — home to millions of city dwellers — are often completely excluded from municipal water networks, forcing residents to rely on costly water vendors or unregulated sources. As the urban population swells, demand for clean water intensifies at a pace that outdated infrastructure simply cannot meet without significant investment and planning.

SOLUTIONS

A range of grassroots and technology-driven solutions are beginning to address Kenya's water crisis. Community borehole projects, rainwater harvesting systems, and solar-powered pumps are bringing clean water to remote villages that national infrastructure has long overlooked. Organizations like Water.org and local NGOs are also investing in affordable household water filters and low-cost sanitation units. Critically, Kenya's government has committed to increasing water sector funding under its "Big Four" development agenda — and when communities are trained to maintain their own water systems, the impact becomes lasting and self-sustaining.
